Vortex Identification from Instantaneous PIV

USC Dryden Wind Tunnel — Provost Fall & Spring Fellowship

Overview

Continuing from the summer Provost Fellowship, this project focuses on extracting quantitative vortex information directly from instantaneous PIV velocity fields acquired over a transitional airfoil. The goal is to identify and characterize the Kelvin-Helmholtz vortices that form in the separated shear layer, enabling estimation of vortex shedding frequencies from spatial data alone without requiring time-resolved acquisition.

Understanding the spatial structure and shedding behavior of these vortices is central to characterizing the separation and reattachment dynamics that acoustic forcing targets. This project provides the algorithmic and analytical tooling to extract that information reliably from the PIV datasets collected in the wind tunnel.

Vortex Identification Pipeline

I developed a Python-based processing pipeline that operates on instantaneous PIV snapshots exported from DaVis. The pipeline computes velocity gradients from the measured velocity field using normalized Gaussian convolution for noise suppression, which reduces sensitivity to PIV interrogation noise while preserving the spatial structure of coherent vortical regions.

Three complementary vortex identification criteria are applied to each snapshot:

Spanwise vorticity (ωz) provides a direct measure of local rotation in the flow and serves as the primary indicator of vortical activity in the shear layer.

Swirling strength (λci), the imaginary part of the complex eigenvalue of the local velocity gradient tensor, isolates regions of swirling motion independently of the local shear, making it more discriminating than vorticity alone in shear-dominated flows like separated boundary layers.

Q-criterion identifies regions where rotation dominates strain, providing a third complementary perspective on the vortex core locations.

Discrete vortex cores are detected by thresholding the λci field and applying connected-component labeling to identify individual vortical structures. Each identified core is assigned a centroid location, enabling measurement of the inter-core spacing along the shear layer.

Frequency Estimation from Spatial Data

With inter-core spacings extracted across multiple instantaneous frames, the pipeline converts spatial separations to temporal frequencies using convection velocity estimates derived from the measured velocity field. This allows estimation of the Kelvin-Helmholtz shedding frequency without requiring time-resolved PIV, working instead from the ensemble of instantaneous snapshots available from phase-locked acquisition.

The output includes statistical distributions of vortex spacing and estimated shedding frequency, providing a quantitative characterization of the shear layer instability that can be compared directly against the acoustic forcing frequencies used in the active control experiments.

Connection to Active Control Work

This vortex identification pipeline directly supports the broader research goal of understanding why certain forcing frequencies are more effective at promoting reattachment. By mapping the natural shedding frequency of the Kelvin-Helmholtz instability from the PIV data, the results provide a physical basis for selecting and interpreting the forcing frequencies tested in the acoustic actuation experiments. Frequencies that resonate with the natural instability are expected to be the most effective at energizing the shear layer, and this pipeline provides the quantitative evidence to test that hypothesis.
