HROne: Long-Range Business Jet Design

AME 261 — Basic Flight Mechanics Aircraft Performance Analysis Lead University of Southern California

Overview

For my AME 261 Basic Flight Mechanics project, I was the Aircraft Performance Analysis Lead and was responsible for the aerodynamic design of a long-range business jet. The project focused on the conceptual and performance-driven aerodynamic design of a next-generation ultra-premium, long-range business jet that must balance high aerodynamic performance with luxury interior features, emphasizing cruise efficiency and field performance.

As the lead, I drove research, analysis, and decision-making for the aircraft's mission performance, aerodynamics, stability, and control.

Aerodynamic Design & Trade Studies

I developed the aircraft's transonic drag model, building the compressible drag framework and the component drag buildup. I tested potential airfoils and wing geometries using ANSYS Fluent CFD to validate performance predictions. Based on these results, I selected a supercritical airfoil and performed sweep and thickness trades to balance wave-drag risks with transonic aircraft performance and structural limits.

I sized the wing and planform area through aspect-ratio and taper trade studies to match the mission requirements for takeoff, landing, climb, and range. Each trade study fed directly into the mission sizing, ensuring that aerodynamic decisions were grounded in real performance constraints.

CFD Validation

To cross-check the analytical performance predictions, I ran CFD simulations in ANSYS Fluent to verify the pressure distributions, flow separation characteristics, and drag estimates at transonic cruise conditions. This provided confidence in the airfoil selection and sweep angle decisions before committing to the final configuration.

Performance Envelope & Mission Sizing

To summarize the aircraft's performance based on the trade studies and design decisions, I generated a full performance envelope, turning-limits diagrams, rate-of-climb diagrams, and power curves. These graph outputs were generated using MATLAB scripts that enabled automation, powerful graphing, and rapid iteration with changes to propulsion, weight, and aerodynamic decisions.

Beyond mission sizing, I tailored our configuration to FAA and CFR gust-load, stall-margin, and field-length requirements, as well as airport-specific constraints. This reflects full ownership of a complex and ambiguous problem, including the ability to break it down into actionable steps across requirements and analysis while managing trade studies.
