Passive and Active Control of Transitional Airfoil Flows at Low Reynolds Numbers

Summer 2025 Provost Fellowship Faculty Advisor: Prof. Geoffrey Spedding USC Dryden Wind Tunnel

Overview

I tested speaker configurations in the Dryden Wind Tunnel to gain a better understanding of active control methods, ultimately designed to enhance the aerodynamic efficiency of an airfoil.

Aerodynamic performance at low Reynolds numbers is governed by changes in boundary layer attachment and flow characteristics that can significantly impact the lift-to-drag ratio and overall efficiency. While separation from the surface degrades lift-to-drag, downstream reattachment can mitigate the loss and improve overall efficiency. One option to facilitate this reattachment is to employ active control methods that create disturbances and excite the flow through forcing. For example, small speakers can be embedded into the airfoil, or external speakers can provide global acoustic forcing. By exciting the shear layer using pre-determined frequencies and amplitudes, it can provide flexibility and control that passive methods may lack.

Objectives

Throughout the summer, the primary method tested was active methods using speakers to answer the question: What frequencies and sound-pressure levels are most effective in improving performance across angles of attack and Reynolds numbers?

The objectives were to: (i) quantify how active controls (internal/external speakers) shift separation and reattachment, and (ii) identify effective ranges in actuation frequency. The study measures lift, drag, and the derived L/D ratio, alongside phase-locked particle image velocimetry (PIV), to estimate separation and reattachment.

PIV Data & Hardware Synchronization

We gathered two primary sets of data over the summer: PIV and force data. PIV tracks particles in an illuminated laser sheet to provide time-resolved maps of the velocity field. This enables us to visualize and characterize the flow over the airfoil, concluding its characteristics and the effects of our control methods.

This data is dependent on a powerful laser, a camera, speakers, and flow visualization (fog), where, before this summer, there was no synchronization between them. It needs to be synchronized not only to ensure repeatability but also to allow us to control the phase differences that enable the acquisition of a broader range of data. Therefore, I implemented a hardware-synchronized trigger system that ties the camera, laser, and speaker to work under the same master clock.

Force Data & Test Automation

Force data was another aspect of the testing procedures that I directly worked with. My work is closely related to the backend of the script; specifically, I redesigned our test automation script. The first difference was the organization of the script; initially, it was a single file, which made it challenging to work on specific parts and complex to understand. Therefore, I organized it into various functions and incorporated different file I/O methods to keep track of our tests. Secondly, I added a feature that allows us to seamlessly run multiple runs in the same configuration, thereby improving the lab's data-gathering efficiency.

Conclusions

Together, the lift curves and phase-locked PIV data demonstrate that acoustic forcing enhances performance, providing both quantitative and visual evidence of earlier reattachment.
