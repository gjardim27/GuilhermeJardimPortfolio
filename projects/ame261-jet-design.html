Long-Range Business Jet Design

AME 261 — Aircraft Performance Analysis Lead

Overview

As part of AME 261 Basic Flight Mechanics, I served as the Aircraft Performance Analysis Lead for a semester-long group project to design a long-range business jet. In this role, I led research, analysis, and decision-making across the aircraft's mission performance, aerodynamics, stability, and control. The project required full ownership of a complex and ambiguous problem: breaking it down into actionable steps across requirements analysis, trade studies, and regulatory compliance, while coordinating with the broader team.

Aerodynamic Framework

I built the compressible drag framework and the component drag buildup for the aircraft. This involved accounting for wave-drag effects in the transonic regime and assembling contributions from each major airframe component into a coherent drag polar. I selected a supercritical airfoil to delay the onset of wave drag and ran sweep angle and thickness-ratio trade studies to balance wave-drag risk with transonic cruise performance and structural load limits.

To validate the aerodynamic predictions, I cross-checked the performance estimates with ANSYS Fluent CFD simulations, using the computational results to confirm that the analytical drag buildup was tracking the correct trends across the cruise Mach range.

Wing Sizing & Planform Trades

I sized the wing and planform area through systematic aspect-ratio and taper-ratio trade studies, targeting mission requirements for takeoff field length, landing field length, climb performance, and range. Each trade involved evaluating the effect on induced drag, wave drag, structural weight, and stall speed simultaneously, requiring careful prioritization of competing constraints.

The final wing configuration was selected to satisfy FAA and CFR stall-margin and field-length requirements while meeting airport-specific compatibility constraints, including wingspan limits and approach speed requirements.

Performance Envelope & Analysis

To summarize the aircraft's performance, I generated a full suite of performance outputs using MATLAB scripts that enabled automation, rapid iteration, and high-quality graphical output. As propulsion, weight, and aerodynamic decisions were updated throughout the design cycle, the scripts allowed the team to regenerate all performance outputs quickly and consistently.

The outputs included: a full performance envelope (service ceiling vs. Mach number), turning-limits diagrams, rate-of-climb diagrams across altitude, and power-required versus power-available curves. These diagrams provided the primary quantitative basis for configuration decisions and demonstrated compliance with the mission requirements for range and cruise altitude.

Regulatory Compliance

Beyond mission sizing, I tailored the configuration to satisfy a range of FAA and CFR requirements, including gust-load factors, stall-margin margins, and both takeoff and landing field-length constraints. Each requirement imposed a constraint on the design space, and managing these constraints simultaneously required a structured approach to the trade studies and a clear understanding of which requirements were active at the final design point.
