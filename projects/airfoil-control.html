Active Control of Transitional Airfoil Flows

Summer Provost Fellowship — USC Dryden Wind Tunnel

Overview

Aerodynamic performance at low Reynolds numbers is governed by changes in boundary layer attachment and flow characteristics that can significantly impact the lift-to-drag ratio and overall efficiency. While separation from the surface degrades lift-to-drag, downstream reattachment can mitigate the loss and improve overall efficiency. One option to facilitate this reattachment is to employ active control methods that create disturbances and excite the flow through forcing. Small speakers embedded into the airfoil, or external speakers providing global acoustic forcing, can excite the shear layer using pre-determined frequencies and amplitudes, offering flexibility and control that passive methods may lack.

This project addresses these questions experimentally in the USC Dryden Wind Tunnel. The objectives are to: (i) quantify how active (internal/external speakers) controls shift separation and reattachment, and (ii) identify effective ranges in actuation frequency. The study measures lift, drag, and the derived L/D ratio, alongside phase-locked particle image velocimetry (PIV), to estimate separation and reattachment locations.

Hardware Synchronization System

PIV requires a powerful laser, a high-speed camera, speakers, and flow visualization (fog) working in concert. Before this project, there was no synchronization between these instruments. Synchronization is critical not only for repeatability but also to control the phase differences that enable acquisition of a broader range of data.

I designed and implemented a hardware-synchronized trigger system that ties the camera, laser, and speaker to a single master clock. This required understanding the timing requirements of each instrument, selecting appropriate signal interfaces, and writing control software to manage the synchronization logic. The result is a system that enables phase-locked PIV acquisition, where each snapshot is captured at a known, repeatable phase relative to the speaker output cycle.

Phase-Locked PIV Results

The synchronization system enables phase-locked PIV plots that illustrate the wall-normal velocity field over the airfoil, where red indicates upward velocity and blue indicates downward velocity. As the speaker phase difference changes across acquisitions, the field exhibits clear evidence that the speaker is outputting noise: disturbances appear over the airfoil surface, particularly visible as red momentum-injection regions at 60° and 90° phase differences.

These regions of injected momentum directly impact the flow and promote reattachment. Crucially, the clarity of these patterns is only possible because the camera, laser, and speaker are hardware-synchronized. Without synchronization, these phase-dependent structures would be smeared out across different acoustic phases and would not be resolvable. The figure thus demonstrates both the effectiveness of acoustic actuation and the capability of the new hardware synchronization method in controlling the experiment.

Test Automation Script Redesign

Force data collection was another key aspect of the testing procedures. The original test automation script was a single monolithic file exceeding 1,200 lines, which made it difficult to isolate specific functionality and challenging for the team to understand or modify safely.

I redesigned the script from scratch. The first major change was organization: I restructured the code into modular functions with clear responsibilities and incorporated structured file I/O methods to track test configurations and results. The second major addition was a multi-run feature that allows seamless execution of multiple runs under the same configuration, significantly improving the lab's data-gathering efficiency without requiring manual intervention between runs.

Force Data & Lift Curve Results

Using the redesigned automation script, we collected lift and drag data comparing airfoil performance with and without acoustic forcing. The lift coefficient vs. angle of attack curves show that the baseline (no speaker) follows the expected near-linear rise at low angles of attack, then exhibits a kink near the critical angle of attack corresponding to the onset of flow separation.

With the speaker active, the lift curve is consistently above the baseline across the tested range, with the most significant improvement near 4° where evidence of flow separation is strongest. This indicates that acoustic forcing energizes the shear layer, promoting earlier reattachment and yielding measurably improved aerodynamic performance. Together, the lift curves and phase-locked PIV data provide both quantitative and visual evidence that acoustic forcing is an effective active flow control strategy for low Reynolds number airfoils.

Reflection

This experience working in the Dryden Wind Tunnel over the summer was more than I could have expected. I gained extensive knowledge about aerodynamic research in a wind tunnel, including collaboration, problem-solving, resilience, and communication. The research I contributed to directly advanced the understanding of low Reynolds number aerodynamics.

Technically, I deepened my proficiency in MATLAB, developed new hardware integration skills, and took direct ownership of two critical infrastructure components of the lab's experimental pipeline: the automation script and the synchronization system. I was also part of Charles Klewicki's dissertation defense, which gave me a view of the full PhD research cycle. This experience opened the door to continuing the work through the Provost Fall Fellowship.
